Welcome to the Poop Olympics! A comparative meta-analysis of fecal dimensionailty across species. Today's competition comprises 215 mammal species from 9 taxonomic orders. With 83 competitors from Africa, 29 from Europe, and 104 from the Americas, it's sure to be a great day for fecal athleticism folks!

Our judges today will be giving out gold, silver, and bronze medals for 3 different competitions:

1. Longest poop

2. Longest poop as a percentage of body length

3. Longest poop in proportion to body mass

In addition to the phylum-wide games, competitions will also run within taxonomic orders. Carnivores (Carnivora), hooved mammals (Ungulata), and rodents (Rodentia) will compete within their own orders. However, because we have such a low number of competitors from the orders MAarsupiala, Pinnipedia, Primata, Xenarthra, Insectivora, and Lagomorpha, those contestants will be merged into one category called "Small Mammals."

Before we begin, we'd like to take a quick moment to thank our sponsors.

This competition was sponsored in part by the 2003 research paper, Terrestrial mammal feces: a morphometric summary and description written Marcia Chame at the Instituto Oswaldo Cruz in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil.

We were also sponsored by the University of Michigan's Animal Diversity Web online database of animal natural history for providing us with the body length and mass data used in some of competitions.

Missing body mass measurements were supplied by the Encyclopedia of Life, and missing body length measurements were supplied by the 2008 research paper Patterns of Body and Tail Length and Body Mass in Sciuridae written by Smith College professor Virginia Hayssen, and by - Ultimate Ungulate - the personal database of Brent Huffman, the Lead Keeper of Mammals at the Toronto Zoo.

With that out of the way...

We'll start off with our first competition - Longest poop. Let's head to our sports analyst, Kat, for our predictions.

Thanks Kat. As we can see in these two graphs of all of our contestants comparing poop length on the y-axis, and body mass and body length respectively on the x-axis, there is a strong correlation between both poop size and body mass, and poop size and body length. Thus, the medals for largest poop are expected to go to our largest competitors today, sure to be a rough competition for our rodent and small mammal competitors! Back to you, Kat.
